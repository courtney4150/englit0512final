"Panopticism" and "Global Frequency"

  • By Courtney Sheridan

Understanding surveillance is becoming increasingly important with the constant presence of the internet and cameras around every single corner. We started with Michel Foucault’s “Panopticism”, which describes a setup called a “Panopticon” with a central tower from which guards could be watching participants. The trick is that the participants never know exactly when the guards are looking at them, so they are forced to assume that they are always being watched even though they are not. The effects of Panopticism that we discussed in class are: internalizations of rules/regulations, rehabilitation rather than punishment, bureaucracy, efficiency, surveillance, specialization, and information society. We have an unusual set of experiences with the Panopticon because of the COVID lockdown. With social distancing, there was always a threat that someone would get “caught” not following the rules, and the penalty for that varied greatly by location. The panopticon is also similar to today’s security cameras; the main difference is that video footage can be stored and recalled later, whereas the Panopticon participants can only be viewed in real time.

We also examined “Global Frequency” by Warren Ellis. This is a comic book that has 2 common characters throughout a series of stories about global surveillance and crime-fighting. There are different artists for each story, which helps the reader distinguish between the stories. After learning about panopticism, we quickly realize that the global frequency is a panopticon. We could also describe it as a humanitarian effort that relies on global surveillance. In class we discussed the premise of the global frequency and whether we would want to have something like this in real life. Following a common argument around surveillance, we landed somewhere around it would be nice to stop crime but we don’t necessarily want to be watched all of the time.
