Hannah Arendt film

  • By Courtney Sheridan

After watching the 2012 movie “Hannah Arendt” about the political theorist of the same name who covers the 1961 Eichmann trial, our class took some time to break down our thoughts on the movie and the connections to the course material. The class thought that the movie was dense and that the subject matter was out of the realm of our studies. Then, we realized it was hard to understand why we were watching this movie; it did not seem to have much to do with technology as we know it today. In fact, though, there were a lot of examples of technology being used to tell a narrative: the typewriters, news articles, and television broadcasting are all being used to shape the world political news surrounding the trial. Hannah’s argument about the trial is also relevant to technology in a less obvious way. Her argument is that not thinking is not human; to be human is to think. This argument is fascinating when applied to artificial intelligence, giving us a new perspective on what it means to be human and how AI can or cannot fit that definition. Are Nazis like AI?

Hannah Arendt also coined the phrase “the banality of evil”, meaning that the greatest evils are not committed by any one villain; it’s the system that allows individuals to contribute to huge evil. This concept can also be applied to technology: for example, social media (as mentioned in The Social Dilemma) can ultimately have poor effects on people, but that is not due to any one person deciding to be “evil”. The banality of evil is important for us to study because of how it will show up in technology-related contexts.
