"Cyborg Manifesto", "R.U.R", and "Blade Runner"

  • By Courtney Sheridan

Also early in the semester, we jumped into some materials about cyborgs and started our course-long discussion about what makes us human. First of all, we needed to define “cyborg”. In our class discussion we define it as something that is part organism and part machine. We talked about what that might look like in our world: do humans who wear contacts, have prosthetic limbs, or use a pacemaker count as cyborgs?

The first reading on this topic was Donna Haraway’s “Cyborg Manifesto”. Haraway described three major boundaries in our society: human/animal, biological/machine, and physical/nonphysical. She claimed that these boundaries are starting to get broken down, which is leading to conflict.

The second reading was “R.U.R” by Karel Capek. One important note about the influence of this piece was that it introduced the word ‘robot’ to English. The word means fully mechanical or “worker”, and came from the word “rab” which meant slave or serf. This can provide a definition for the present-day use of the word robot. In class, we discussed how a “mechanical worker” can be an accurate definition for a robot.

In addition to the two readings, we watched most of the 1982 film “Blade Runner”. One of the main topics of conversation was about the characters who were unaware that they might be replicants. This led to a discussion over what it means to be human in a world of cyborgs. We wondered how it would feel to have thought your whole life that you’re a human, but then you might find out one day that you’re a replicant (or an AI)? What happens then?

The combination of these three pieces of media led us to ask some open ended questions. Where do we draw a line between human and machine? Who deserves rights? What rights? We feel comfortable saying that ChatGPT isn’t alive - but how would we know if it was alive? Should AI have voting rights if it claims that it’s alive? In respect to the “R.U.R.” reading: What does a robot want? Would they turn against humans? What would they be motivated to do against humans? How does this help us reflect on our society?
