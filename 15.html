“Warcraft and Utopia" and "We are the Gold Farmers"

  • By Courtney Sheridan

Through Alexander Galloway’s work, we can further explore the world of World of Warcraft. In the first piece, “Warcraft and Utopia”, Galloway presents some definitions of utopias and challenges the reader to consider whether or not World of Warcraft should be considered a utopia. In class, we discussed: What do we consider a utopia? Is World of Warcraft a utopia? Why/why not? I think that at first, it seems like the game is obviously a utopia because it is this beautiful idealized world. However, when asked if we would live there, the class got quiet. Galloway helped us further discuss this by presenting two definitions of utopia: the first is life after capitalism; the second is utopia as nostalgia. For the first idea, we discussed how we couldn’t really imagine a life after capitalism. Possibly the only way would be if everything was able to be 3D-printed and therefore free, and a capitalist system would not function. Secondly, we discussed the idea of utopia being before capitalism. The system we had before capitalism was feudalism, and we wouldn’t really consider that a utopia.

In the second piece, “We are the Gold Farmers”, Galloway explores the implications of the characters’ races in World of Warcraft. For example, you can’t play the troll in World of Warcraft without getting the Jamaican accent. I noticed my character, a mage, dances in a style that reminds me a lot of Hawaiian hula. Galloway wonders why we even need to have races in a game like World of Warcraft. Does having different races impact the play of the game?
