Dynamics: The State of the Art (GDC Talk)

  • By Courtney Sheridan

Starting off what I viewed as the second half of the class, we watched Clint Hocking’s GDC Talk called “Dynamics: The State of the Art”. This brought about a thought-provoking conversation about dynamism, what games are about, and how to define authorship of a video game. Unlike other mediums, games are tied to the players themselves. The game can be about whatever the players think it’s about; it might be designed for one purpose, but the players contribute meaning as well, which ultimately can change what the game is about. The amount of agency for the player depends on the genre of the game. In film editing, the film is about decisions made by directors in what they choose to show the viewer (and what to not show). Therefore, the meaning of the film comes from the conclusions viewers make based on editing.

When it comes to authorship of video games, there are a couple of different parties involved. First, there are designers and makers of the game’s mechanics. Secondly there are the players themselves. We discussed that it makes sense to always include the designers/makers in some capacity as the authors of the game. However, the complicated part is the discussion about players having authorship of their own game experience. One can argue that since the experience and narrative of the game can be different for every player based on the decision they make, they help author the story. It is difficult to define this, and even when giving the player some authorship of the game, it’s hard to know how much they truly author. This topic has already started to reach major court cases, so this definition will likely continue to fluctuate as we have more debate and more examples brought to court.
