“Don’t Hate the Player, Hate the Game” and “Games Without Play”

  • By Courtney Sheridan

In Liz Nakamura’s piece “Don’t Hate the Player, Hate the Game”, she discussed the racialization of labor in World of Warcraft. This piece specifically introduced the idea of Chinese gold farmers in World of Warcraft: the tendency of players in China to play the game basically as a full time job so that they can sell gold to players elsewhere in the world. The role of Chinese players “resembles that of other immigrant groups who cross national borders in order to work, but unlike other types of ‘‘migrant’’ workers, their labors are offshore, and thus invisible*they are ‘‘virtual migrants” (page 131). This has created an extremely negative view of Chinese players and increased racially divided play.

In David Golumbia’s “Games Without Play”, he argues that Role-Playing Games (RPGs) don’t give the player that many options in the games. The article cites games like Half-Life and Halo that just guide the user to killing specific targets, and I think that World of Warcraft falls into this category as well. In World of Warcraft, the player is really restricted to killing, walking around, and interacting with a limited number of items and Non-Playable Characters (NPCs). This piece challenges what it means to “play” a game and questions whether games like World of Warcraft truly fit that definition. As I started to play World of Warcraft for this class, I found that it did feel like just completing tasks as they are assigned to me. I had some agency in which quests to accept and in what order to complete them, but I grew weary of having to walk somewhere, kill 10 of some creature, and then walk back to the quest-giver.
